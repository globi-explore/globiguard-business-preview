# 03. Case studies

## 1. Cloudflare

### What it looked like

A CDN.

### What it became

A global connectivity, security, and control plane.

### Why that matters

Cloudflare did not stop at "we accelerate websites." It kept moving toward:

- identity
- traffic control
- policy
- tunnels
- Zero Trust
- developer platform

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Do not define yourself as a point tool.  
Define yourself as the **place where governed execution policy is applied across the estate**.

The sidecar or SDK is your edge surface.  
The real business is the authority behind it.

---

## 2. Stripe

### What it looked like

A payments API.

### What it became

Financial infrastructure with control, compliance, orchestration, and risk built in.

### Why that matters

Stripe's SDKs are not the moat. The moat is:

- merchant relationships
- financial rails
- risk systems
- billing logic
- dashboard and operational tooling

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Your libraries should make adoption easy, but the business should live in the **operational system behind the integration**.

If customers rely on GlobiGuard for:

- key management
- approval workflows
- audit evidence
- policy operations

then the SDK is just the door.

---

## 3. Sentry

### What it looked like

An error SDK.

### What it became

A developer incident and visibility workflow system.

### Why that matters

The SDK is everywhere, but customers pay for:

- hosted ingestion
- triage workflow
- project control
- alerting
- collaboration
- operational memory

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Instrument once, monetize the workflow around the signal.

For GlobiGuard, that workflow is:

- review
- override
- escalation
- policy tuning
- evidence

---

## 4. n8n

### What it looked like

A workflow builder with many nodes.

### What it became

An automation operating environment with control, deployment choice, governance, and production workflows.

### Why that matters

n8n proves that "open" does not kill monetization if the product expands into:

- cloud
- enterprise operations
- governance
- production team workflows

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Do not be just "the node package."  
Be the layer that makes automation **safe to run in production**.

---

## 5. PostHog

### What it looked like

A frontend analytics snippet.

### What it became

A product data, experimentation, and developer analytics operating system.

### Why that matters

PostHog kept the SDK easy, but expanded into the system behind it:

- project identity
- dashboards
- experiments
- replay
- feature flags

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Start with instrumentation and integration, but capture the **control and insight layer**.

---

## 6. OpenClaw

### What it looks like

A personal assistant product.

### Why it is still useful as a reference

Its onboarding and runtime packaging show an important pattern:

- distribution surface
- guided setup
- persistent runtime
- control layer behind channels

### Lesson for GlobiGuard

Onboarding is part of the product.  
Do not ship raw components and expect the market to assemble the business for you.

## Summary

The repeated pattern is this:

1. the visible surface looks smaller than the real business
2. the winner owns the control plane, not just the edge artifact
3. open distribution does not kill monetization when the workflow, operations, and trust system stay central
